Integration by Parts — Complete Step-by-Step Guide

For AQA · Edexcel · OCR · WJEC  |  Year 12 & 13 · Pure 2

Integration by parts is one of the most important techniques in A-Level Maths. It appears on every exam board and is essential for Year 13 Pure. This guide walks through the formula, the ILATE selection rule, and fully worked examples — exactly as you'd present them in an exam to earn all method marks.

The Integration by Parts Formula

Integration by parts is derived from the product rule for differentiation. If you have an integral of the form ∫ u dv, you can rewrite it as:

∫ u dv = uv − ∫ v du

In Leibniz notation, this is written as:

∫ u (dv/dx) dx = uv − ∫ v (du/dx) dx

You need to choose which part of the integrand to call u and which to call dv/dx. The ILATE rule helps with this choice.

The ILATE Rule — Choosing u and dv/dx

ILATE is a priority order for choosing u (the part you differentiate). Pick the first type that appears in your integrand:

  1. I — Inverse trig (arcsin, arccos, arctan)
  2. L — Logarithms (ln x, log x)
  3. A — Algebraic (x², 3x, polynomials)
  4. T — Trigonometric (sin x, cos x, tan x)
  5. E — Exponential (eˣ, aˣ)

The remaining part becomes dv/dx, which you integrate to find v.

Exam tip: Logarithms almost always become u. You cannot integrate ln x directly, so it must be differentiated. Similarly, polynomials paired with exponentials: make the polynomial u so it reduces to zero after differentiating.

Worked Example 1 — Polynomial × Exponential

Find ∫ x eˣ dx

1
Choose u = x (algebraic before exponential) and dv/dx = eˣ M1
2
Differentiate u: du/dx = 1, so du = dx
3
Integrate dv/dx: v = eˣ B1
4
Apply the formula: ∫ x eˣ dx = x·eˣ − ∫ eˣ·1 dx M1
5
Evaluate the remaining integral: = x eˣ − eˣ + C A1 cao
6
Factorise (preferred form): = eˣ(x − 1) + C

Worked Example 2 — Logarithm × Polynomial

Find ∫ x² ln x dx

1
Choose u = ln x (logarithm before algebraic) and dv/dx = x² M1
2
Differentiate: du/dx = 1/x
3
Integrate: v = x³/3 B1
4
Apply formula: ∫ x² ln x dx = (x³/3)·ln x − ∫ (x³/3)·(1/x) dx M1
5
Simplify: = (x³ ln x)/3 − ∫ x²/3 dx
6
Integrate: = (x³ ln x)/3 − x³/9 + C A1
7
Factorise: = (x³/9)(3 ln x − 1) + C A1 cao

Applying Integration by Parts Twice

Some integrals require integration by parts applied twice — for example ∫ x² eˣ dx. After the first application you'll get ∫ x eˣ dx, which itself needs parts again.

Cyclic integrals: For ∫ eˣ sin x dx, applying parts twice brings you back to the original integral. Let I = ∫ eˣ sin x dx, apply parts twice, then solve for I algebraically. This is a classic exam question — learn it.

Definite Integration by Parts

For definite integrals, apply the limits to the uv term and the remaining integral separately:

[uv]ₐᵇ − ∫ₐᵇ v (du/dx) dx

Always substitute the limits into the uv term first before evaluating the integral. Show every substitution clearly — mark schemes award method marks for this.

Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

⚠ Forgetting the constant of integration — always write + C for indefinite integrals, even in intermediate steps if you're adding them up.
⚠ Wrong choice of u — if you choose u = eˣ with dv/dx = x, you'll get a harder integral. Always follow ILATE.
⚠ Sign errors — in ∫ u dv = uv minus ∫ v du. The minus sign is the most common source of lost marks.

Exam Board Differences

  • AQA & Edexcel: Integration by parts is in Year 13 Pure 2. Expect it paired with ln x, eˣ sin/cos, and definite integrals. Cyclic integrals appear at grade A/A* level.
  • OCR A & OCR MEI: May require proving the formula from the product rule — know the derivation.
  • WJEC: Tabular integration (the "ladder" method) is allowed but you must show working clearly.

Practice Question

Try this yourself before checking the answer:

Find ∫ 3x sin(2x) dx

Hint: u = 3x (algebraic), dv/dx = sin(2x) (trig). You'll need to integrate sin(2x) to get v = −cos(2x)/2.
